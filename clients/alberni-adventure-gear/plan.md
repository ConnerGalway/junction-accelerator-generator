# **Where Are We Going?**

## **Goal**

One year in, you are doing what most year-one businesses are not. Bookings have come in steadily since launch, your Google Business Profile is active, and your website is already ranking number one organically for *camping gear Port Alberni* and showing up on the first page for *camping gear Vancouver Island*. European travellers heading to Tofino and Ucluelet are finding you on Google and renting clean MEC gear instead of flying with their own kit. You have close to thirty Google reviews, a stable booking system on Bookable after a long setup, and 500 to 600 new Instagram followers from a single giveaway with Bella Pacifica.

The work ahead is about converting visibility you already have into more booked rentals, and putting the measurement underneath it so you can see what is actually doing the work. Three things need attention. First, the website is tracking nothing at all: neither Google Analytics nor the Meta Pixel is installed, which means every visitor who lands and leaves disappears, and every grant dollar you spend on ads this summer will spend blind. Second, when a visitor lands on your gear page, the path to booking is doing more work than it should. The fact that people are emailing you to ask whether you rent individual items is a signal that the page is asking them to hunt. Third, you have a $1,500 marketing grant on the table and an open question about where to put it. This plan addresses all three at once.

The plan asks for steady weekly time, not a second job. May and early June are foundation work: tracking, page fixes, and the first of a small library of local guide pages that earn search traffic the way Carmen's blog posts earn it for Glacier Raft Company in Golden. From late June into July, $500 of the grant goes into a Google Ads test, followed by a $500 Meta test in August. The remaining $500 holds in reserve to scale whichever channel pays for itself, and the September Toy Run weekend lands at the end of the plan as the kind of single-day revenue moment that can become an annual recurring line if the system supports it.

> **Your Goal**
> Turn the visibility you have already earned into more bookings, measure what is actually working, and head into your second full season with a system you can run yourself in the hours you have.

## **Strategic Positioning**

Mountain Equipment Co-op and Sports Junkies sell gear; you rent it. The competition that actually matters is the inconvenience of flying with bulky camping gear across an ocean, the cost of buying gear for one trip, and the gamble of arriving in Tofino hoping a rental shop still has stock. Thames Valley Outfitters in Ontario, the business you have studied as a model, scaled by being the obvious answer for travellers who do not want to own gear they need for one trip a year. That position is open to you on Vancouver Island, and your location is what makes it defensible. Anyone driving from the Nanaimo ferry to Tofino or Ucluelet passes through Port Alberni. That story is missing from the website, the ads, and the partnership content. It should live in all three.

> **Your Strategic Positioning**
> Alberni Adventure Gear is the easy yes for travellers heading to the west coast who do not want to fly with their gear, buy it for one trip, or gamble on what is left in Tofino. Clean, quality kit, ready when they arrive, from someone who actually answers the phone.

# **How Will We Get There?**

## **Tactic 1: Tracking, Website Conversion, and Local SEO Pages**

### **What We Heard From You**

The website is working. Organic search is the biggest source of inbound interest. The booking system on Bookable is finally stable after a difficult eight-month setup with a contractor who left things in a worse state than she found them. Reviews are coming in steadily through your post-rental email. What is missing is measurement, and a few specific friction points on the site that are costing bookings. Visitors are emailing to ask whether you do individual rentals because the gear page is structured around bundles. There is appetite for more content that helps travellers plan their trip through to the west coast, but you have already tried a Squarespace blog and it looked dated within weeks. The priority is something more flexible than a blog.

### **What This Is**

A three-part foundation project. The first piece is tracking installation: GA4 and the Meta Pixel, plus basic conversion events so every dollar of ad spend that comes next can be measured. The second is a set of targeted improvements to the homepage and gear page so visitors find what they came for in fewer clicks. The third is a small library of evergreen local guide pages published as standalone pages rather than blog posts, designed to capture search traffic from people planning trips to central and west Vancouver Island. Each piece feeds the others. Tracking measures whether the conversion fixes are working, the new pages bring in qualified traffic, and the fixes themselves turn that traffic into bookings rather than emails.

### **Why It Matters**

The $1,500 grant cannot be measured without tracking installed. There is no way to compare Google Ads to Meta Ads, see which keywords convert, or know whether the new content pages are pulling their weight. The conversion fixes matter because the email volume is telling you the page is leaking. The local guides matter because you are already at the ceiling of what your homepage alone can rank for. Three months from now, with all three in place, you have the infrastructure for everything that follows, and the work itself costs mostly time spent in May and early June, before peak season pulls your attention elsewhere.

### **What Success Looks Like**

| Metric | 30 Days | 60 Days | 90 Days |
|---|---|---|---|
| Google Analytics 4 installed | Live and tracking sessions | Bookable click event firing as a conversion | At least 30 days of clean data for ad measurement |
| Meta Pixel installed | Live and firing on all pages | Custom audience of past 30-day visitors built | Audience large enough to retarget (500+ users) |
| Inbound email questions about individual rentals | Tracked baseline | Reduced by half | Reduced by 75% or more |
| New local guide pages published | 1 page live | 3 pages live | 5 pages live, at least one indexed in Google top 10 |
| Google reviews | 35+ | 45+ | 60+ on the path to 100 by end of season |

### **Implementation Plan**

The work is sequenced so nothing waits on anything else for longer than it needs to. Tracking goes in first because every measurement downstream depends on it. The gear page restructure and the "How to get here" addition happen in parallel because both are short fixes Squarespace makes easy. The local guides start in Week 3 and continue through the rest of the plan at a pace of one new page per fortnight. Each component feeds the tactics that follow: the Pixel feeds the Meta retargeting audience, the conversion fixes turn ad clicks into bookings, and the local guides expand the search footprint that Google Ads can complement instead of duplicate.

#### Part A: Install Tracking

1. **Install Google Analytics 4 on the Squarespace site.**
   * In Squarespace, open Settings → Developer Tools → External API Keys → Google Analytics
   * Create a Google Analytics 4 property at analytics.google.com using the same Google account that owns your Business Profile
   * Paste the GA4 Measurement ID into Squarespace
   * Verify it is firing using the GA4 Realtime report by visiting the site in a separate browser

2. **Install the Meta Pixel on the Squarespace site.**
   * Create a Meta Business account at business.facebook.com if you do not already have one
   * Create a new Pixel inside Events Manager and copy the Pixel ID
   * In Squarespace, paste the Pixel ID into Settings → Marketing → Facebook Pixel
   * Verify it is firing using the Meta Pixel Helper Chrome extension

3. **Set up basic conversion tracking.**
   * In GA4, mark the Bookable click as a conversion event
   * In Meta Events Manager, set up a "Lead" event for the contact form submission and a "ViewContent" event for the gear page
   * Do not wait for these to be perfect. The point is data flowing now, refinement later.

#### Part B: Improve Conversion on the Existing Site

4. **Add a "How to Get Here" link to the top navigation.**
   * In Squarespace, edit the main navigation and add a new link labelled "How to Get Here"
   * The page itself should be short: one paragraph explaining where Port Alberni is on the route to Tofino and Ucluelet, an embedded Google Map, and brief driving directions from Nanaimo and Victoria
   * Add a single primary call to action at the bottom linking to the Bundles page

5. **Restructure the gear page so individual rentals are visible above the fold.**
   * At the top of the page, add three or four tile-style entry points: Camping Bundle, Beach Bundle, Backpacking Bundle, Individual Rentals
   * Each tile should be a photo with a short label, clicking through to that section or a dedicated subpage
   * Add a short line of intro copy: "Not sure what you need? Scroll down or contact us and we'll put together a custom kit."
   * This solves the "do you do individual rentals" email problem head-on

6. **Fix the homepage text contrast.**
   * The white text over the hero image is hard to read against the lighter areas of the photo
   * In Squarespace, apply a text shadow or a semi-transparent dark band behind the headline and CTA button
   * Test on a phone in bright sunlight to confirm legibility

7. **Replace the plain-text email address on the contact page with the contact form only, or with an obfuscated email image.**
   * The assessment flagged plain-text email addresses as a spam risk and you have already mentioned getting spam
   * If the contact form is now your preferred path, remove the plain-text email entirely

#### Part C: Build the Local Guide Page Library

8. **Brainstorm twenty post ideas using ChatGPT as a partner.**
   * Open ChatGPT and prompt it with: "You are a young family planning a one-week trip to Vancouver Island in July, driving from Nanaimo to Tofino. List 20 things you would Google before and during the trip."
   * Repeat with a European backpacker and a retired couple in an RV as separate personas
   * Pick the five strongest ideas across all three lists. Strong means high search intent, naturally connects to gear rental, and stays useful year after year.

9. **Add a "Local Guides" link to the top navigation.**
   * The link goes to a simple index page that displays each guide as a tile with a photo and title
   * Each guide is a standalone page on the website, not a blog post. This gives you more layout control and avoids the dated-look problem you ran into with the Squarespace blog template.
   * Five starting ideas:
     - "Your Last Stop Before Tofino: What to Pack for a West Coast Camping Trip"
     - "Where to Camp Near Tofino and Ucluelet"
     - "What to Do in Port Alberni on Your Way Through"
     - "Vancouver Island Road Trip: A One-Week Itinerary with Gear Rentals Built In"
     - "Backpacking the West Coast Trail: The Gear You Actually Need"

10. **Write the first guide using ChatGPT as a research and outlining partner, not as the writer.**
    * Use the prompt: "I'm writing a guide called [title] for travellers heading to Vancouver Island. I run a camping gear rental business in Port Alberni. Give me an outline with section headings, the keywords each section should target, and three questions a traveller would have for each section."
    * Use the outline as a scaffold. Write the prose yourself so the voice stays yours.
    * End every guide with a clear next step: a button linking to the relevant bundle page on your site.

11. **Publish one guide per fortnight for the rest of the 90-day window.**
    * Add each new guide to the Local Guides index page as it goes live
    * Share it on Instagram and Facebook the day it publishes
    * Link to it from at least one other page on the site (the homepage, a bundle page, or another guide)

### **Tips**

* The single most important hour of this entire plan is the morning you install GA4 and the Pixel. Without them, everything else is guessing. Block a specific morning on your calendar, sit down with coffee, and do not get up until your own visit shows up in the GA4 Realtime report.
* When you write the local guides, do not over-optimise. Carmen's posts at Glacier Raft Company still rank four years later because they answer real questions in plain English. Write the way you talk to renters at pickup.
* Do not turn the Local Guides into a blog. A blog that goes a month without an update starts to look like the business closed. Standalone evergreen pages do not have that problem.
* When ChatGPT suggests a title with "Ultimate Guide" in it, change it. Travellers Google specific questions, not ultimate guides.
* Test the gear page restructure on your phone before publishing. The page needs to make sense on the screen most travellers are using.

## **Tactic 2: Google Ads**

### **What We Heard From You**

You have $1,500 in grant money in the account, an open question about whether to spend it on Google or Meta, and no prior experience with paid ads. The honest answer to Google or Meta is both, in that order, and only after tracking is live. Your business has a structural advantage on Google that Meta cannot match. Someone searching "camping gear rental Tofino" has already worked out they need what you sell. They are looking for a vendor, not browsing for ideas. The job of the ad is to put your business in front of them at that moment with a clear path to booking.

### **What This Is**

A Search-only Google Ads campaign built in Expert Mode rather than Smart Mode, with $500 of grant money spread across two to three weeks. The campaign targets a tight set of high-intent keywords your website is not yet ranking number one for, sends clicks to the Bundles page, and is measured against actual Bookable clicks. The campaign does not include Display, Performance Max, or YouTube. Search is the only ad format that captures buying intent the way this business needs.

### **Why It Matters**

For a business with limited time and a small budget, Search ads are the only paid channel with a real chance of paying for themselves in the first month. Display and Performance Max spend the budget faster and produce mostly impressions and unqualified clicks. Search lets you put a dollar amount on a specific search term, see what it returned in bookings, and decide whether to keep spending on that term or kill it. Run carefully, $500 should produce enough data to make a clear call on whether the remaining $500 reserve goes back into Google or shifts to Meta.

### **What Success Looks Like**

| Metric | 30 Days | 60 Days | 90 Days |
|---|---|---|---|
| Google Ads account in Expert Mode | Account created, Smart Mode prompt skipped | First campaign live | Campaign optimised based on Search Terms report |
| Search keywords targeted | 8 to 12 keywords, single themed ad group | Negative keywords list built from Search Terms report | Top three performing keywords identified |
| Click-through rate on Search ads | Above 3% within first 7 days of spend | Above 5% after first round of headline testing | Above 6% on the best-performing ad |
| Cost per click | Under $2.00 | Under $1.50 | Under $1.25 on the best keywords |
| Bookings attributed to Google Ads | At least 1 booking confirmed by month-end | At least 3 bookings confirmed | Clear answer to whether to scale spend |

### **Implementation Plan**

The campaign is built in three short phases. Setup happens in Week 4, after tracking has been live for at least two weeks. The campaign launches in Week 5 at $35 per day for two weeks, which spends roughly the $500 grant allocation. Week 7 is the decision point: based on what the data shows, the remaining $500 reserve either goes back into Google or shifts to Meta. The whole tactic depends on Tactic 1 being in place. Without tracking, the campaign cannot be measured.

#### Part A: Set Up the Account

1. **Create a Google Ads account at ads.google.com using your existing Google account.**
   * Use the same Google account that owns your Business Profile and the new GA4 property. This makes linking later much easier.
   * On the first screen, click "Switch to Expert Mode" at the bottom. Do not run a Smart Campaign. Smart Mode gives Google control over targeting in ways that work for large budgets and rarely for small ones.

2. **Link Google Ads to your Google Analytics 4 property.**
   * In Google Ads, go to Tools → Linked Accounts → Google Analytics
   * Select your GA4 property and confirm the link
   * This is what lets you see which keywords produced actual sessions and Bookable clicks

3. **Link Google Ads to your Google Business Profile.**
   * In Google Ads, go to Tools → Linked Accounts → Google Business Profile
   * This enables location extensions, which add your business address and phone number to the ad and improve click-through rates for nearby searches

#### Part B: Build the Campaign

4. **Create one Search campaign with one ad group.**
   * Campaign objective: Leads
   * Campaign type: Search only. Untick Display Network and Search Partners.
   * Budget: $35 per day. Most days will spend less. The daily figure is a cap.
   * Bidding: Maximize Clicks with a maximum CPC bid cap of $2.00 for the first two weeks. After two weeks, switch to Maximize Conversions if at least 15 conversions have been recorded.
   * Location: British Columbia and Alberta. Skip Washington and Oregon for the first campaign because currency and border friction reduce conversion rates.
   * Language: English

5. **Add 8 to 12 keywords in phrase match.**
   * Suggested starting list: "camping gear rental Vancouver Island," "camping gear rental Tofino," "tent rental Tofino," "camping equipment rental BC," "backpacking gear rental Vancouver Island," "rent camping gear Pacific Rim," "Tofino camping rental," "Ucluelet camping gear"
   * Skip "Port Alberni" keywords. You already rank number one organically.
   * Use phrase match (quotation marks around each keyword) for the first campaign. Phrase match is tight enough to keep spend qualified and loose enough to surface variants you would not have thought of.

6. **Write one Responsive Search Ad with 8 to 12 headlines and 3 descriptions.**
   * Headlines should test different angles: location ("Last Stop Before Tofino"), problem ("Don't Fly With Your Gear"), proof ("MEC Tents, Ready When You Arrive"), and urgency ("Book Your Summer Rental Now")
   * Descriptions should mention clean gear, pickup in Port Alberni, and booking online
   * Final URL: send all traffic to the Bundles page rather than the homepage. Bundles is where the booking action happens.

7. **Add Sitelink, Callout, and Call extensions.**
   * Sitelinks: How It Works, Partners, About, Contact
   * Callouts: "Clean Quality Gear," "MEC Tents," "Easy Pickup in Port Alberni," "Free Local Cancellation"
   * Call extension: your business phone number (250 720 7901), enabled during your working hours

#### Part C: Manage and Decide

8. **Check the Search Terms report twice a week.**
   * Google Ads → Insights and Reports → Search Terms
   * Any search term that produced clicks but is clearly off-topic (such as "free camping gear" or "camping gear for sale") gets added as a negative keyword
   * Any search term that produced bookings and is not already in your keyword list gets added

9. **Pause underperforming keywords after 14 days.**
   * Any keyword with 50+ clicks and zero conversions gets paused
   * Any keyword with zero clicks at all after 14 days has either too low a bid or too narrow a match type. Look at the bid first.

10. **Make the spend decision at the end of Week 7.**
    * Look at three numbers: total spend, Bookable clicks generated, bookings confirmed
    * If Google Ads is producing bookings at a cost per booking below your average gear rental margin, the remaining $500 reserve goes back into Google Ads
    * If the campaign is not paying for itself, the reserve shifts to Meta. The call is made from the data, not from a hunch.

### **Tips**

* The biggest mistake first-time Google Ads users make is leaving Smart Campaigns on by accident. The moment you see a recommendation to "let Google optimise your budget across more channels," ignore it. You want manual control while you are learning what works.
* Google will repeatedly nudge you to add Performance Max. Decline every time for the first 90 days. Performance Max spends fast and is opaque about where the spend went.
* Bookable does not have a direct conversion event you can import into Google Ads, so for now use the Bookable click as your conversion. It is a proxy, but a tight one. A server-side conversion from Bookable to Google can be set up later.
* Do not run ads during the September Toy Run weekend. Those eight camping bundles are sold direct through the event organisers. Paid clicks during that window would burn money on demand you already have.
* You do not need perfect ad copy. Google's machine learning is shockingly good at picking the right headline combinations once it has enough data. Write 10 reasonable headlines and let the system test them.

## **Tactic 3: Meta Ads and Partnership Amplification**

### **What We Heard From You**

Instagram is your largest social channel and the one you spend the most time on. A single giveaway with Bella Pacifica drove 500 to 600 new followers, which is real evidence the audience responds when the right content reaches them. You tried to set up a Meta ad before this coaching call and hit a payment-method error that has not been resolved. Beyond paid spend, the partnership network has assets that have not been deployed: the May photoshoot at Bella Pacifica is sitting unused, reciprocal 15% discount agreements with Hello Adventures and Triple Peak Sauna are in place but almost no customers know about them, and there are active conversations with content creators that have not been formalised. The plan uses the grant to test paid Meta, and uses the partnership network to make that paid spend work harder than it would on its own.

### **What This Is**

A small paid Meta campaign run after Google Ads has a few weeks of data, plus a structured approach to partnership content that gives the paid spend more to work with. The campaign uses $500 of the grant over two weeks and is measured directly against the Google Ads campaign. The partnership component costs nothing in ad spend. It is a routine for capturing content from the photoshoots, collaborations, and creator visits you are already doing, and putting that content to work in both organic posts and the paid ads themselves.

### **Why It Matters**

Meta is where your existing audience already lives, which makes retargeting valuable even if the cold campaign performs only moderately. The Pixel installed in Tactic 1 is what makes retargeting possible. Without it, every ad campaign runs cold to a cold audience. Partnership content is a force multiplier in paid social: ads built from real creator and partner content consistently outperform studio-style brand creative, and the content you are already producing for collaborations is exactly the kind of content that works. Skipping this tactic leaves grant money on the table and leaves the partnership network underused.

### **What Success Looks Like**

| Metric | 30 Days | 60 Days | 90 Days |
|---|---|---|---|
| Meta Business Suite payment issue resolved | Resolved | n/a | n/a |
| Meta Pixel firing on the Squarespace site | Live (set up in Tactic 1) | Custom audience of 30-day visitors built | Audience size 500+ users |
| Paid Meta campaign launched | Account ready | Campaign live | Two weeks of spend complete |
| Cost per click on Meta | n/a | Under $0.80 | Under $0.60 |
| Bookings attributed to Meta | Baseline | At least 2 bookings | Clear answer on whether to scale or shift budget |
| Creator/partner content captured | At least one shoot logged | 3+ creator or partner pieces in the content library | Library deep enough to run a second campaign without new shoots |

### **Implementation Plan**

The campaign is sequenced to start two weeks after Google Ads, which spreads the workload and gives the Pixel time to accumulate enough data for a useful retargeting audience. The partnership content workflow runs in parallel from Week 1 because the May Bella Pacifica photoshoot is happening regardless, and the content needs to be captured properly so it can feed both organic posts and ads. The campaign itself uses cold targeting (interest-based) and warm retargeting (Pixel-based) in a single split to balance reach and efficiency.

#### Part A: Resolve the Account and Confirm Pixel

1. **Resolve the existing Meta Business Suite payment issue.**
   * Go to business.facebook.com → Billing → Payment Methods
   * Remove and re-add your card. If the issue persists, contact Meta Business Support through the help chat with your business verification documents ready.
   * Do not try to launch an ad before this is sorted. It will fail and waste a morning.

2. **Confirm the Meta Pixel installed in Tactic 1 is firing on every page.**
   * Use Meta Pixel Helper Chrome extension to test the homepage, bundles page, and contact page
   * In Events Manager, confirm at minimum PageView and ViewContent events are firing

3. **Build the retargeting audience in Meta Ads Manager.**
   * Audiences → Create Audience → Custom Audience → Website
   * Define audience: people who visited any page on alberniadventuregear.ca in the last 30 days
   * Save the audience. It will populate as visitors come in. Hold it back from any ad set until it has at least 500 people.

#### Part B: Build the Content Workflow

4. **Create a simple content library in Google Drive or Dropbox.**
   * Folders: Photoshoots, Creator Content, Partner Collabs, UGC from Customers
   * Every shoot, every customer-submitted photo, every creator post you collaborate on gets saved here within a week of being captured
   * This is the well you draw from for ads and organic posts. The library matters more than any single piece of content.

5. **Capture the May Bella Pacifica photoshoot with paid ads in mind.**
   * Shoot vertical (9:16) and square (1:1) versions of every setup. Meta ads need vertical for Reels and Stories, square for feed.
   * Capture lifestyle shots that show people using the gear, not just product on its own. Lifestyle outperforms product in paid social by a wide margin.
   * Get at least one short video clip of someone setting up a tent, even if it is 10 seconds. Video ads outperform static almost across the board.

6. **Set a partnership content rhythm.**
   * Once per quarter, schedule one creator collaboration: free gear rental in exchange for content and a post on their channel
   * Tourism Port Alberni is the best resource for finding local creators. Ask their team for a shortlist of creators in the 5,000 to 50,000 follower range who post outdoor and travel content.
   * Treat the content creators produce as your highest-priority library asset. A real creator's video used as a paid ad almost always outperforms anything made in a studio for the same purpose.

#### Part C: Run the Paid Campaign

7. **Create a single Meta campaign with two ad sets.**
   * Campaign objective: Sales (formerly Conversions). The optimisation event is the Bundles page view, since you do not yet have a server-side conversion from Bookable.
   * Budget: $35 per day across the campaign for 14 days, splitting roughly $250 to cold and $250 to retargeting
   * Ad Set 1 (Cold): Audience defined by interests: camping, hiking, Vancouver Island travel, MEC, Tofino, Pacific Rim National Park. Geographic targeting: Canada (excluding BC residents within 50km of Port Alberni). Age 25 to 55.
   * Ad Set 2 (Retargeting): Audience is the 30-day visitor custom audience built in Step 3. Wait to launch this ad set until the audience reaches 500 users.

8. **Create three to four ads using your best partnership and creator content.**
   * Each ad should have one short headline, one line of body copy, and a clear CTA button ("Book Now" or "Learn More")
   * Lead with the strongest hook: "Don't fly with your camping gear," "Last stop before Tofino," "Clean MEC tents ready when you arrive"
   * Use the videos and lifestyle photos from the content library rather than stock-style brand imagery

9. **Check the campaign every two days.**
   * Ads Manager → Campaign view → Filter to "Last 7 days"
   * Watch three metrics: cost per click, click-through rate, and conversions (Bundles page views)
   * Frequency above 4 in 7 days means the audience is saturated. Rotate the creative or expand the audience.

10. **Compare to Google Ads at the end of Week 9.**
    * Pull both reports side by side: total spend, clicks, conversions, cost per conversion
    * The remaining $500 reserve goes to whichever channel had the lower cost per booking, scaled to extend that channel's spend for another two to three weeks
    * Do not split the reserve. Concentrating budget on the winner produces better data than dividing it across both.

### **Tips**

* When you work with a content creator, do not ask them to make an ad. Ask them to make their normal content using your gear. Real creator content used as an ad almost always outperforms creator content made to look like an ad.
* The 15% reciprocal discount with Hello Adventures and Triple Peak Sauna is undermarketed. Mention it on your gear page, in your Instagram bio, and in the post-rental email. It is a differentiator that costs you nothing extra.

## **Quick-Hit Recommendation: Google Business Profile Polish**

Worth doing during the foundation weeks. Your Profile is already claimed and active, but the assessment and the coaching call surfaced four items that will compound the benefit of the other tactics.

* **Change business hours to Flexible.** Walk-up traffic is not your model, and the current 9-6 hours discourage email enquiries outside those windows.
* **Fill in every remaining field.** Profile strength is at 70%. The missing fields are likely accessibility attributes, additional categories, and service area boundaries. Fill in every field where the answer is actually yes.
* **Check the Q&A section twice a month.** Questions get posted there and stay unanswered unless you respond. Random users will answer with bad information if you do not get there first.
* **Set a goal of 100 reviews by end of summer 2026.** You are at 30. Around 100 is the threshold where Google starts treating your Profile as a regional authority and showing it for broader searches like "camping gear Vancouver Island," beyond just searches that include your business name.
